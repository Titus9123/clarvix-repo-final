Quick guide

How to read Israel-related claims online

Use this page as a calm checklist before sharing, arguing or trusting a viral claim.

01 · Source

Who is making the claim?

Look for original documents, named institutions, transparent methodology and date. Treat anonymous screenshots and context-free clips as incomplete until verified.

02 · Language

Is the wording factual or dehumanizing?

Claims that blame all Jews, deny Jewish peoplehood, glorify harm, or use conspiracy language are warning signs of antisemitic framing.

03 · Distinction

Criticism is not automatically antisemitism

Policy criticism can be legitimate. It becomes antisemitic when it targets Jews as Jews, applies classic antisemitic tropes, or denies rights only to Jews collectively.

Antisemitism pattern checklist

Common online patterns to watch

Pattern A

Conspiracy replacement

A political or social event is explained by secret Jewish control rather than evidence.

Pattern B

Collective blame

Jews worldwide are blamed for actions of a government, army, group or individual.

Pattern C

Double standard without principle

Israel is judged by a unique rule that is not applied to any comparable state or conflict.

Pattern D

Erasure and denial

Jewish history, identity, indigeneity, suffering or security concerns are dismissed as fake by default.

Start with dates, actors and verified sources

This MVP intentionally avoids graphic shock content. It gives a structure for future sourced timelines and explainers.

Current events

What happened?

Record date, location, named actors and original source. Add updates only when a reliable source changes the record.

Claim check

What is being claimed?

Separate direct evidence, interpretation and opinion. Mark unknowns instead of filling gaps with assumptions.

Impact

Who is affected?

Discuss civilians, communities and security concerns without dehumanizing any group.
